# Understanding Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is a structured, time-limited, evidence-based
form of psychotherapy that helps people identify and change unhelpful thought
patterns and behaviors. It is built on the idea that our thoughts, feelings, and
behaviors are connected: shifting one can change the others.

## Core techniques

- **Cognitive restructuring**: noticing automatic negative thoughts and testing
  them against evidence.
- **Behavioral activation**: scheduling small, rewarding activities when
  motivation is low.
- **Exposure**: gradually and safely facing feared situations to reduce anxiety.
- **Thought records**: writing down the situation, the thought, the emotion, and
  a more balanced alternative.

## What it helps with

CBT is widely used for anxiety, low mood, stress, sleep difficulties, and
unhelpful habits. A typical course runs 8-20 sessions, with practice between
sessions being the most important part.

## Self-help starting point

When you notice a difficult feeling, pause and ask: "What am I telling myself
right now?" Write it down, then note one kinder or more realistic alternative.
This is not a substitute for professional care — if distress feels unmanageable,
reach out to a qualified clinician or a local crisis service.
